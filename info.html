Why do we ignore disasters
until it's too late?

The greatest threat to human survival isn't just climate change or ecological collapse—it's human psychology. Our brains are hardwired with cognitive biases that make us blind to latent signals.

Normalcy Bias

The psychological phenomenon where people underestimate both the likelihood of a disaster and its potential impact. Because a catastrophic event hasn't happened in our recent memory, our brain falsely assumes that "things will continue to function normally as they always have."

The Danger: This bias causes communities to ignore a 300% salinity spike in their drinking water because "the water has always been fine before."

Survivorship Bias

The logical error of concentrating on the people or systems that survived a past event, while inadvertently overlooking those that didn't because of their lack of visibility. This leads to a false sense of security and overconfidence in current infrastructure.

The Danger: "Our seawall survived the 2010 storm, so we are safe," completely ignoring that sea levels have risen and the offshore coral reefs that absorbed wave energy have since died.
